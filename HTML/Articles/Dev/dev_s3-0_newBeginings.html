#dev_3-00 Season 3: New beginnings 13 June 2022

It’s that time again. I have burnt my old site down. It is now time for this site rise out of the ashes better, stronger, and more refined than ever before. But how will I do this? What does it even mean? Why is it necessary?

A new vision

The previous site wasn’t bad – but it has some major flaws. Most of which were behind the scenes, with bad repo organisation, over complicated CSS and disorganised JavaScript. Although most of these problems appeared to be behind the scenes, they often fund weird ways of casing their shadow on the presentation if the site and limiting what I could actually do.

By the end of the last iteration, I had set up a semiautomated contend population system and automatic custom html template creation. Both systems had potential as powerful development tools: however, my system was not designed to capitalise on them.

It’s time a build and new HTML structure that favours such systems and allows for simple and repeatable setup.

But why?

As a young engineer-to-be I have learnt that good designers, engineers, and problem solvers are often lazy. This ‘laziness’ allows one to do more with less time, effort, and resources. If I make a site that is easy to develop and maintain then I will have more time to polish and produce quality content.

Let’s look at my current blog section. To add a new blog post I need to format its contents as semantic HTML is one file. Then I still need to create a separate file to act as a container for said contents, and finally I need to add links to both to a JavaScript list so that I can create index sections. There are more combination and complexities to it but that basic trade-off was for perceived efficiency of product over efficiency of design. Both concepts are equally important, but my solution sat of the fence and thus solved neither.

How will the new one be better?

I will be made for automation from square one. One of the biggest issues with my old site was that I made automation and repetition o structure inherently hard because they were originally finnicky and fragile. For example, my CSS classes often changes dramatically based on the context they were used in or the other classes they were combined with. At the time of writing the CSS it made sense to me – but I was way too complicated ot keep track of. As a result, I ended up repeating CSS so that I could get the same effect as elsewhere without worrying about permutations. Developing was doing the same setup and grunt work over and over again each time.

I strive to make the new site cleaner, better organised and easier to expand and maintain. This goes for CSS, JavaScript, HTML, and general repo organisation. It’s not just about planning to be minimalistic and clean – that’s always one’s intention. It’s about documenting what do so that I’m not confused by it later.

I’ve always wanted to do that, but documentation felt tedious. And it will be. But I know it’s also going to be worth it later. If I add to this dev-log more regularly I will not become the herculean chore it has grown into in the past.

And speaking of the past… It’s not just organisation of assets I should consider but organisation of time. One of my biggest failures has been failing to implement everything I wanted to because of deadlines. I fool myself into thinking I am progressing because I am working on the site. But, without clear deadlines or goals, work often flounders around in circles, leaving me with little to show for my efforts. Sure, I learn – but I’m not just learning, I’m making something here. And I need to make it as well as it can be.

Before I start designing, I need to design what and when I will design, and that’s coming up next.
